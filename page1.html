MEIOSIS

Mitosis is the basic concept of cell division; it’s used widely throughout the body. Meiosis uses the same concepts except it’s just a bit more complicated.

See, meiosis is still a form of cell division, but it’s not used for the basic cells of the body. This is a specific kind of division that will ultimately produce your gametes, sperm or eggs. On this page, we will specifically go through the basics of meiosis. This includes the phases, and what exactly goes on in those phases. 

KEY TERMS:
            01) Diploid - Having two complete sets of chromosomes in one cell (2n); ex. in humans, a                                                                        cell with 46 chromosomes.
            02) Haploid - Having one complete set of chromosomes in one cell (n); ex. in humans, a                                                                           cell with 23 chromosomes, a sperm or an egg.
            03) Homologous - having the same alleles or genes in the same order of arrangement                                               (on chromosomes.)

Meiosis is separated into two sections, Meiosis I and Meiosis II. The goal of meiosis one is to separate homologous chromosomes, or chromosomes that are similar in length and structure. Meiosis one will result in two diploid cells, or containing 46 chromosomes. 

Before any of this happens though, the cell undergoes interphase

  1. PROPHASE I - Chromatin, or DNA, coils into chromosomes, homologous chromosomes pair up (synapsis), microtubules (spindle fibers) begin to form once centrioles migrate to opposite ends of the cell, and the nuclear membrane will start to dissolve. During synapsis, crossing over or recombination occurs. This is when the homologues will exchange corresponding segments of DNA to create a more unique code.
  2. METAPHASE I - Homologues (homologous chromosomes) migrate to the equator of the cell, spindle fibers attach to the chromosomes.
  3. ANAPHASE I - Homologous chromosomes are separated as they are pulled to opposite sides of the cell.
  4. TELOPHASE I & CYTOKINESIS - The membrane reforms around the groups of chromosomes, forming two nuclei. The cell then splits into two diploid daughter cells.
Once the two diploid daughter cells have formed, Meiosis I has been completed. The main goal of separating homologous chromosomes has been completed. 
Now we are ready to move on to Meiosis II. The second half of meiosis is essentially the same thing, except the goal now is to separate the sister chromatids from the current diploid daughter cells. This will result in four haploid cells total. It should be noted the cells we currently have will not undergo interphase again after Telophase I and cytokinesis, they will advance to Prophase II right away. 
  1. PROPHASE II - Chromatin, or DNA, coils into chromatids, homologous chromatids pair up (synapsis), microtubules (spindle fibers) begin to form once centrioles migrate to opposite ends of the cell, and the nuclear membrane will start to dissolve. There will be no crossing over or recombination during this step.
  2. METAPHASE II - Homologues migrate to the equator of the cell, spindle fibers attach to the chromatids.
  3. ANAPHASE II - Homologous chromatids are separated as they are pulled to opposite sides of the cell.
  4. TELOPHASE II & CYTOKINESIS - The membrane reforms around the groups of chromatids, forming four separate nuclei. The cell then splits into four haploid daughter cells.

Once the final division has happened and we are left with four cells containing 23 complete chromosomes, we have finished the whole process of meiosis.  Depending on what is being created, there will be four resulting cells but the outcome will differ from sperm to eggs.

                            - If eggs are being created, one egg will be present along side three polar bodies, or cells that won't develop into eggs.

                            - If sperm are being created, all cells will be sperm cells.
