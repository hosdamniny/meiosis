GAMETOGENESIS

As some sort of living thing, we can't help but create more of our species. The way we create is all dependent on gametogenesis.
Gametogenesis is the process of producing gametes. This process includes both mitosis and meiosis, and differ slightly from the two types. Gametogenesis can be separated in to spermatogenesis and oogenesis; one for creating sperm and the other for eggs, respectively. we'll start with spermatogenesis.

KEY TERMS:
            O1) Spermatogenesis - the process of producing sperm cells.
            02) Oogenesis - the process of producing egg cells.

SPERMATOGENESIS

Spermatogenesis starts in the outerlining of the seminiferous tubule, which is located inside the testes. As the cell develops more and more, it will move toward the center of the tubule. Sperm cells start out as initial germ cells that will form stem cells, which will then divide mitotically to form spermatogonia, early development stage sperm cells. 

Once the spermatogonium has mitotically divided enough times, it will form into the primary spermatocyte and begin meiosis.

The cell will then be recognized as a secondary spermatocye after the first meiotic division; a spermatid after the second.

These spermatids will finally form into full fledged sperm after the final division. This ultimately completes spermatogenesis.

OOGENESIS

Oogenesis is similar to spermatogenesis in terms of steps. Although there are some key differences:
  • Only one of four cells will develop into a mature gamete in oogenesis.
  • Mitotic divisions are done before birth, and mature gametes cease to be produced around the age of 50.
  • Oogenesis is interrupted at specific times and continue once specific precursors are present.
Oogenesis starts in the ovaries as the primordial germ cells produce oogonia, or early development stage eggs. They will then mitotically divide to produce the primary oocytes and are paused in prophase of meiosis I until sexual maturity.

Once the individual has reached sexual maturity, the primary oocyte will progress to a secondary oocyte and be paused in metaphase of meiosis II. At this point we have one polar body (which may or may not divide again) and one haploid cell after the first meiotic division.

Because polar bodies do not form into mature eggs, we only have to look at the secondary oocyte now. Once the secondary oocyte exists, the individual will begin ovulating. This is commonly called a period. This process entails the thickening of the uterine lining with blood where the sec. oocyte will implant itself and wait to be or not to be fertilized by a sperm cell.

If the egg is successfully fertilized by the sperm cell, it will become an ovum and will follow through with the final stages of meiosis II. This will ultimately develop into a fetus and eventually an individual.
